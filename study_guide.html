☀️ Chapter 6: Photosynthesis

Acquiring Energy from the Sun · Chloroplasts · Light Reactions · Calvin Cycle · Photorespiration
⚗️ The Master Equation — KNOW THIS COLD!
Photosynthesis Overall Equation
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light Energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Carbon Dioxide + Water + Light → Glucose + Oxygen
⭐ Key Facts About Photosynthesis
  • Endergonic reaction — REQUIRES energy input (light) to run
  • Takes place in chloroplasts (found in plant cells and algae)
  • Produces glucose (food) and releases O₂ as a byproduct
  • The OPPOSITE of cellular respiration
🧠 Memory Trick

"Plants COOK with sunlight." Ingredients go IN (CO₂ + H₂O + light), food comes OUT (glucose). The O₂ we breathe is just the exhaust from cooking. Endergonic = needs energy = like cooking needs heat.

"THYLAKOID = LIGHT · STROMA = SUGAR"
THY-lakoid = THY light reactions (yours = light). STROMA = STROng sugar factory. Light hits the thylakoid → energy goes to stroma → sugar is made.

⭐ Key Light Facts
  • Blue light has MORE energy than red light (shorter wavelength = higher energy)
  • Green light is the LEAST effective for photosynthesis — it is reflected, not absorbed
  • Chlorophyll ABSORBS red and blue light; REFLECTS green
  • Light wavelength is measured in nanometers (nm)
🧠 Memory Trick — Which light is strongest?

"BLUE = BETTER" — Blue light has a shorter wavelength and MORE energy. Think of a blue flame (hotter) vs. a red flame (cooler). Blue = more powerful = more energy for photosynthesis.

💡 Stage 1: Light-Dependent Reactions
📖 Definition

Light-dependent reactions = the first stage of photosynthesis that uses light energy to split water and produce ATP and NADPH. Occurs in the thylakoid membrane.

⭐ Reactants & Products of the Light Reactions
REACTANTS (go IN):
  • 💧 Water (H₂O)
  • ☀️ Light energy
  • ADP + Pᵢ (to make ATP)
  • NADP⁺ (to make NADPH)
PRODUCTS (come OUT):
  • ⚡ ATP (energy carrier)
  • ⚡ NADPH (hydrogen carrier)
  • 💨 O₂ (released as gas — the O₂ we breathe!)
⚡ Photosystems I & II — The Light Reaction Machinery
⚠️ Confusing but Important!

Photosystem II comes BEFORE Photosystem I in the actual sequence. They were named in the order they were discovered, not the order they work!

Step Photosystem II (PSII) Electron Transport Chain Photosystem I (PSI)
What happens Light hits chlorophyll → water is SPLIT → O₂ released → electrons energized Energized electrons pass through carrier molecules → protons pumped into thylakoid → ATP made via chemiosmosis Electrons re-energized by light → passed to NADP⁺ → forms NADPH
Products O₂ (released) + energized electrons ATP (via ATP synthase) NADPH
📖 Key Terms — Light Reactions
  • Chemiosmosis = protons flow through ATP synthase (like water through a turbine) to generate ATP
  • ATP synthase = the protein channel that protons flow through; makes ATP as they pass
  • Photophosphorylation = using light energy to add a phosphate to ADP → making ATP
  • NADPH = energy carrier that carries hydrogen atoms to the Calvin cycle
  • Photolysis = splitting of water by light energy in PSII
🔄 Stage 2: The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
📖 Definition

Calvin Cycle = the second stage of photosynthesis; uses ATP and NADPH from the light reactions to convert CO₂ into glucose. Occurs in the stroma. Also called C₃ photosynthesis because the first product is a 3-carbon molecule (G3P).

⭐ Reactants & Products of the Calvin Cycle
REACTANTS (go IN):
  • 🌬️ CO₂ (from air through stomata)
  • ⚡ ATP (from light reactions)
  • ⚡ NADPH (from light reactions)
PRODUCTS (come OUT):
  • 🍬 G3P → Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)
  • ADP + Pᵢ (returned to light reactions)
  • NADP⁺ (returned to light reactions)
⭐ The 3 Steps of the Calvin Cycle
  1. Carbon Fixation: CO₂ is attached to RuBP (5-carbon) by the enzyme RuBisCO → makes a 6-carbon compound that immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules (3-PGA)
  2. Reduction: ATP and NADPH are used to convert 3-PGA into G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate) — the actual sugar product
  3. Regeneration of RuBP: Most G3P is used to regenerate RuBP (using ATP) so the cycle can continue; some G3P exits to make glucose
🧠 Memory Trick — Calvin Cycle

"CATCH · REDUCE · RECYCLE"
1. CATCH the CO₂ (carbon fixation by RuBisCO)
2. REDUCE it to sugar (G3P) using ATP + NADPH
3. RECYCLE the RuBP so the cycle keeps going

🌵 Section 6.4: Photorespiration — Putting the Brakes on Photosynthesis
📖 Definition

Photorespiration = a wasteful process where RuBisCO accidentally grabs O₂ instead of CO₂, producing a 2-carbon compound that must be broken down (wasting ATP). It reduces photosynthesis efficiency by up to 25%.

⭐ Why Does Photorespiration Happen?
  • RuBisCO can bind to EITHER CO₂ or O₂ — it's not perfectly selective
  • When O₂ levels are high (hot, dry days when stomata close), RuBisCO grabs O₂ instead of CO₂
  • This produces a 2-carbon molecule instead of G3P — a waste product
  • The plant must spend ATP to break down this waste product
  • Net result: less glucose made, more ATP wasted
⭐ C₃ vs. C₄ vs. CAM Plants — Solutions to Photorespiration
Plant Type Examples Strategy Best Climate
C₃ Plants Wheat, rice, soybeans, most trees Standard Calvin cycle — first product is a 3-carbon molecule (3-PGA) Cool, moist climates — photorespiration is less of a problem
C₄ Plants Corn, sugarcane, crabgrass Pre-fix CO₂ into a 4-carbon molecule in mesophyll cells, then deliver concentrated CO₂ to bundle sheath cells where Calvin cycle runs — keeps O₂ away from RuBisCO Hot, sunny climates — very efficient
CAM Plants Cacti, succulents, pineapple Open stomata at NIGHT to collect CO₂; store it; run Calvin cycle during the day with stomata CLOSED (saves water) Hot, dry deserts — water conservation is key
🧠 Memory Trick — C₃ vs. C₄ vs. CAM

"3 = Normal · 4 = Efficient · CAM = Night Owl"
C₃ = regular plants (3-carbon first product). C₄ = corn and sugarcane (4-carbon trick to avoid photorespiration). CAM = cactus (opens stomata at night to save water — like a night shift worker).

🎓 Professor's TopHat Questions — Chapter 6
Which wavelength of light is most effective for photosynthesis?
✅ Blue and red light (NOT green — green is reflected)
Chlorophyll absorbs blue and red light most strongly. Green light is reflected, which is why plants look green.
Where does the Calvin cycle occur?
✅ In the stroma of the chloroplast
Light reactions = thylakoid membrane. Calvin cycle = stroma. Know both!
What is the role of water in the light reactions?
✅ Water is split (photolysis) to provide electrons to replace those lost from PSII, and O₂ is released as a byproduct

⚡ Chapter 7: Cellular Respiration

How Cells Harvest Energy from Food · Glycolysis · Krebs Cycle · ETC · Fermentation
⚗️ The Master Equation — KNOW THIS COLD!
Cellular Respiration Overall Equation
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 36 ATP
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy (ATP)
⭐ Key Facts About Cellular Respiration
  • Exergonic reaction — RELEASES energy (stored in glucose) as ATP
  • Occurs in the cytoplasm + mitochondria
  • ALL living cells do cellular respiration (plants too!)
  • The OPPOSITE of photosynthesis
  • Do NOT confuse with breathing — cellular respiration is a chemical process inside cells
⭐ Photosynthesis vs. Cell Respiration — They're OPPOSITES!
Photosynthesis Cell Respiration
Uses CO₂ + H₂O + LightUses Glucose + O₂
Produces Glucose + O₂Produces CO₂ + H₂O + ATP
Endergonic (needs energy)Exergonic (releases energy)
Occurs in chloroplastsOccurs in cytoplasm + mitochondria
Only in plants/algaeIn ALL living cells (plants too!)
🧠 Memory Trick

"Respiration is Photosynthesis BACKWARDS." The reactants of one are the products of the other. They're like breathing in and breathing out — two sides of the same coin.

📍 WHERE Does Each Step Happen? — MAJOR EXAM TOPIC
⚠️ This is a MAJOR exam question — know the locations!

The professor specifically asks: "Where in the mitochondria does glycolysis occur? What about cell respiration or fermentation?" Know the exact location of every step!

1️⃣ Glycolysis — Splitting Glucose
📖 Definition

Glycolysis = "glucose splitting" — the breakdown of 1 glucose molecule into 2 pyruvate molecules. Occurs in the cytoplasm. Does NOT require oxygen. Happens in ALL cells (aerobic and anaerobic).

⭐ Glycolysis Summary
REACTANTS (go IN):
  • 1 Glucose (6-carbon)
  • 2 NAD⁺
  • 2 ADP + 2 Pᵢ
PRODUCTS (come OUT):
  • 2 Pyruvate (3-carbon each)
  • 2 NADH (electron carriers)
  • 2 ATP (net gain)
🧠 Memory Trick — Glycolysis

"Glycolysis = 2 for 2" — 1 glucose splits into 2 pyruvates, and you get 2 ATP. Everything is in 2s! Also: "Glyco = GLUCOSE · Lysis = SPLIT" — it literally means glucose splitting.

2️⃣ Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA (Transition Step)
⭐ What Happens in the Transition Step
  • Each pyruvate (3-carbon) loses a CO₂ → becomes a 2-carbon acetyl group
  • The acetyl group attaches to CoA → forms Acetyl-CoA
  • NAD⁺ is reduced to NADH (electron carrier)
  • CO₂ is released as a gas (part of the CO₂ you exhale!)
  • Occurs TWICE (once per pyruvate — 2 pyruvates from glycolysis)
  • Location: mitochondrial matrix
3️⃣ Krebs Cycle — Extracting More Energy
📖 Definition

Krebs Cycle (also called the Citric Acid Cycle) = a series of reactions in the mitochondrial matrix that breaks down Acetyl-CoA, releasing CO₂ and producing electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂) and a small amount of ATP.

⭐ Krebs Cycle — Per 2 Acetyl-CoA (full cycle, both turns)
REACTANTS:
  • 2 Acetyl-CoA (2-carbon each)
  • Oxaloacetate (recycled — the "starter")
PRODUCTS:
  • 4 CO₂ (released as gas)
  • 6 NADH (electron carriers)
  • 2 FADH₂ (electron carriers)
  • 2 ATP
⚠️ CO₂ is produced in TWO places!

CO₂ is released during: (1) the Transition step (pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA) AND (2) the Krebs cycle. This CO₂ diffuses out of the cell and is eventually exhaled. This is where the CO₂ in your breath comes from!

🧠 Memory Trick — Krebs Cycle

"Krebs = Carbon Recycler" — The Krebs cycle strips carbons off Acetyl-CoA as CO₂ and loads the energy onto NADH and FADH₂ "buses" to carry to the ETC. Think of it as a carbon-stripping factory that charges up energy buses.

4️⃣ Electron Transport Chain (ETC) — The Big ATP Factory
📖 Definition

Electron Transport Chain = a series of protein complexes in the inner mitochondrial membrane. NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons, which release energy used to pump protons (H⁺) across the membrane, driving ATP synthesis via chemiosmosis.

  1. NADH and FADH₂ donate their electrons to the ETC
  2. Electrons pass through protein complexes, releasing energy at each step
  3. Energy is used to pump H⁺ ions into the intermembrane space (building up concentration)
  4. H⁺ ions flow back through ATP synthase (like water through a turbine)
  5. ATP synthase uses that flow to produce 34 ATP
  6. At the END of the chain, electrons + H⁺ combine with O₂ → H₂O (water!)
⚠️ What happens if O₂ is not available?

Without O₂, electrons cannot be accepted at the end of the ETC → the chain STOPS → NADH and FADH₂ cannot unload their electrons → they pile up → Krebs cycle stops too → cell switches to FERMENTATION!

🧠 Memory Trick — ETC

"The ETC is a Proton Pump Waterfall" — Electrons flow downhill through the chain (releasing energy), that energy pumps protons uphill (building pressure), then protons flow back downhill through ATP synthase (making ATP). Just like a dam generating electricity from water flow!

5️⃣ Fermentation — When There's No Oxygen
📖 Definition

Fermentation = an anaerobic (no oxygen) process that regenerates NAD⁺ from NADH, allowing glycolysis to continue. Produces only 2 ATP (just from glycolysis). The Krebs cycle and ETC do NOT run during fermentation.

⭐ WHY does fermentation happen?

Without O₂, NADH cannot unload its electrons at the ETC. NADH piles up. Glycolysis NEEDS NAD⁺ to keep running. Fermentation converts NADH back to NAD⁺ by dumping the electrons onto pyruvate — this keeps glycolysis going and keeps the cell alive (barely).

Type What Pyruvate Becomes Products Real-World Example
Alcoholic Fermentation Pyruvate → Ethanol + CO₂ Ethanol (alcohol) + CO₂ + 2 ATP Yeast making beer, wine, bread (CO₂ makes bread rise!)
Lactic Acid Fermentation Pyruvate → Lactic acid Lactic acid + 2 ATP Your muscles during intense exercise; yogurt, cheese, sour cream
🧠 Memory Trick — Fermentation Types

"YEAST = ALCOHOL · MUSCLES = LACTIC ACID"
Yeast makes beer → alcoholic fermentation → ethanol + CO₂ (CO₂ makes bread rise!)
Your muscles burn during a sprint → lactic acid fermentation → that burning feeling IS lactic acid!

🍔 Section 7.6: Other Food Molecules — Fats & Proteins
⭐ Glucose is NOT the Only Fuel — Fats and Proteins Can Also Be Used!
Molecule Where It Enters Respiration Key Point
Fats (lipids) Broken into glycerol + fatty acids → fatty acids enter as Acetyl-CoA (Krebs cycle) Fats produce MORE ATP per gram than glucose — that's why fat is such a good energy storage molecule
Proteins Broken into amino acids → amino acids enter at various points (glycolysis, pyruvate, Krebs cycle) Proteins are used as fuel only when carbohydrates and fats are depleted (e.g., starvation)

⭐ The Two Laws of Dieting (from textbook 7.6)
  • Law 1: All calories are equal — a calorie from fat = a calorie from carbs = a calorie from protein
  • Law 2: (calories in) − (calories out) = fat stored
  • Fad diets (Atkins, paleo) work only by reducing total calories — not because of any special metabolic magic
🎓 Professor's TopHat Questions — Chapter 7
Where does glycolysis occur?
✅ In the cytoplasm (cytosol) — NOT in the mitochondria!
Glycolysis happens BEFORE the mitochondria get involved. It's the only step that occurs in the cytoplasm.
What is the net ATP yield from glycolysis?
✅ 2 ATP (net)
Glycolysis uses 2 ATP to start and produces 4 ATP — net gain = 2 ATP.
What happens to NADH if oxygen is not available?
✅ NADH cannot donate electrons to the ETC → fermentation occurs to regenerate NAD⁺
Without O₂, the ETC stops. NADH piles up. Fermentation converts NADH back to NAD⁺ so glycolysis can continue.
What is the final electron acceptor in the ETC?
✅ Oxygen (O₂) — it combines with electrons and H⁺ to form water (H₂O)

🔬 Chapter 8: Mitosis & Cell Division

Cell Cycle · Binary Fission · Chromosomes · PMAT · Cytokinesis
🔄 The Cell Cycle — Overview
⭐ The Cell Cycle vs. Mitosis — They Are NOT the Same!

Cell cycle = the ENTIRE life of a cell (interphase + mitosis + cytokinesis)
Mitosis = just the nuclear division phase of the cell cycle (PMAT)

Phase Stage What Happens DNA Status
INTERPHASE
(most of cell's life)		 G₁ (Gap 1) Cell grows; makes proteins and organelles; normal cell activities Normal (2n)
S (Synthesis) DNA is REPLICATED — every chromosome is copied; cell now has double the DNA Being copied → 4n
G₂ (Gap 2) Cell continues to grow; prepares for division; checks DNA for errors 4n (doubled)
MITOSIS PMAT (4 phases) Nucleus divides; chromosomes are separated into 2 identical sets Separating

🧠 Memory Trick — Cell Cycle Order

"I Pee on the MAT" = Interphase → Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase → Cytokinesis

⚠️ TopHat Question — G₂ chromosome number!

If an organism has 36 chromosomes in G₂, what is the diploid number? Answer: 36 — The diploid number = the chromosome count. In G₂, DNA has been replicated (so there are 72 chromatids) but the chromosome NUMBER is still 36 (each chromosome has 2 sister chromatids joined at the centromere). Count centromeres = count chromosomes!

🧬 Chromosomes — Key Vocabulary
📖 Key Chromosome Terms — Know the Differences!
  • Chromosome = a single, condensed DNA molecule + proteins (histones). Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
  • Chromatin = the loose, uncondensed form of DNA + histone proteins (what chromosomes look like during interphase)
  • Sister chromatids = the two IDENTICAL copies of a chromosome joined at the centromere AFTER DNA replication. They are exact copies of each other.
  • Homologous chromosomes (homologues) = a PAIR of chromosomes — one from mom, one from dad — that carry genes for the same traits but may have different versions (alleles). NOT identical copies.
  • Centromere = the region where sister chromatids are joined together. Count centromeres to count chromosomes!
  • Diploid (2n) = having 2 sets of chromosomes (one from each parent). Human body cells = 2n = 46.
  • Haploid (n) = having 1 set of chromosomes. Human gametes (sperm/egg) = n = 23.
  • Karyotype = a photograph of all the chromosomes in a cell, arranged in pairs by size and shape. Used to detect chromosomal abnormalities.
⚠️ Sister Chromatids vs. Homologous Chromosomes — VERY Common Exam Confusion!
Sister Chromatids Homologous Chromosomes
IDENTICAL copies of the same chromosomeSame chromosome TYPE but from different parents (may have different alleles)
Joined at the centromereNOT joined — separate chromosomes
Created by DNA replication (S phase)Inherited — one from mom, one from dad
Separated during ANAPHASE of mitosisSeparated during meiosis I (not mitosis)
📖 Chromosome Structure
  • Histones = positively charged proteins that DNA wraps around; help compact DNA
  • Nucleosome = DNA wrapped around 8 histone proteins (like beads on a string); every 200 nucleotides
  • Human chromosomes are ~40% DNA and ~60% protein
  • If stretched out, DNA from one chromosome = ~5 cm (2 inches) long!
🧠 Memory Trick — Sister vs. Homologous

"Sisters = Identical Twins · Homologues = Fraternal Twins"
Sister chromatids = identical twins (exact same DNA, joined at the hip). Homologous chromosomes = fraternal twins (same chromosome type, but from different parents — look similar but aren't identical).

🦠 Binary Fission — Prokaryote Cell Division
📖 Definition

Binary fission = how prokaryotes (bacteria) divide. The circular DNA is replicated, then the cell simply splits in two. Much simpler than mitosis because prokaryotes have less DNA and no nucleus.

⭐ Binary Fission Steps
  1. The circular chromosome is replicated
  2. The two chromosomes move to opposite ends of the cell
  3. The cell elongates
  4. A new cell wall forms in the middle, splitting the cell into two identical daughter cells
⭐ Why is Eukaryotic Division More Complex?
  • Eukaryotes have MUCH MORE DNA (humans: ~3 billion base pairs vs. bacteria: ~4 million)
  • DNA is longer and must be carefully packaged into chromosomes
  • DNA is wrapped around histones and organized into chromatin
  • Must use a spindle apparatus to pull chromosomes apart precisely

⚠️ Major Exam Topic — Know each phase in DETAIL

The professor says: "Be able to describe, in detail, what is occurring in the four steps of mitosis." This WILL be on the exam.

Animals pinch themselves in half (cleavage furrow = like a belt tightening). Plants can't pinch because of their rigid cell wall, so they BUILD a new wall down the middle (cell plate).

🎯 Outcome of Mitosis
⭐ What Do You Get from Mitosis?

  • End with: 2 diploid cells (2n) — genetically IDENTICAL to the parent cell
  • Used for: growth, repair, asexual reproduction
  • Chromosome number is PRESERVED (does NOT change)

🎗️ Chapter 8: Cancer

What Is Cancer · Oncogenes · Tumor Suppressors · Staging · Treatments
🔬 What Is Cancer?
📖 Definition

Cancer = a disease caused by mutations in genes that control cell division. Cancer cells divide uncontrollably, do not respond to normal "stop dividing" signals, and can invade other tissues.

⭐ Key Facts About Cancer
  • ~40–50% of Americans will be diagnosed with cancer in their lifetime
  • Cancer is caused by mutations in genes that regulate cell division
  • Most cancers require multiple mutations to develop (not just one)
  • Cancer cells do NOT respond to normal cell cycle checkpoints
  • Cancer cells can be immortal — they keep dividing indefinitely
🧬 Two Types of Cancer-Causing Gene Mutations
⚠️ Major Exam Topic — Know both types and the GAS/BRAKES analogy!
Gene Type Normal Function When Mutated Analogy
Proto-oncogene → becomes Oncogene Promotes cell division when needed (like a gas pedal) Stuck in the "ON" position → cell divides non-stop even without a signal 🚗 Stuck gas pedal — car accelerates even when you're not pressing it
Tumor Suppressor Gene Stops cell division when needed (like brakes) Brakes are broken → cell can't stop dividing 🚗 Broken brakes — car can't stop even when you press the brake pedal
⭐ Specific Tumor Suppressor Proteins — HIGH YIELD!
Protein Normal Function Cancer Connection
p53 Inspects DNA integrity; stops cell division if DNA is damaged; triggers DNA repair or apoptosis (cell death) 50% of ALL cancers have a disabled p53. 70–80% of lung cancers have mutant p53 (benzo[a]pyrene in cigarette smoke mutates p53).
Rb (Retinoblastoma protein) Blocks E2F protein, which prevents DNA copying. When Rb is released, E2F enables DNA replication. 40% of ALL cancers have a defective Rb. Without Rb, E2F is always active → constant DNA replication → uncontrolled division.
Telomerase inhibitor Inhibits telomerase, which means chromosomes lose telomere tips with each division → limits cell to ~30 divisions Almost ALL cancers destroy the telomerase inhibitor → cancer cells can divide indefinitely (immortal cells)
🧠 Memory Trick — p53 and Rb

"p53 = Police · Rb = Red light"
p53 is the DNA police — it checks for damage and arrests the cell cycle if damage is found. Rb is a red light — it stops the cell from copying DNA. When either is broken, the cell runs red lights and ignores the police → cancer.

📖 Key Cancer Vocabulary
📖 Must-Know Terms
  • Tumor (neoplasm) = an abnormal mass of cells resulting from uncontrolled cell division
  • Benign tumor = does NOT invade surrounding tissue and does NOT spread; NOT cancer (but can still cause problems by pressing on organs)
  • Malignant tumor = CAN invade surrounding tissue and spread to other parts of the body; IS cancer
  • Metastasis = cancer cells break away from the original tumor, enter the bloodstream or lymph system, and form NEW tumors in other parts of the body. This is what makes cancer deadly.
  • Angiogenesis = tumors send signals to grow NEW blood vessels toward themselves, stealing oxygen and nutrients from normal cells
  • Apoptosis = programmed cell death — a normal process where damaged or unneeded cells kill themselves. Cancer cells often disable apoptosis.
  • Carcinogen = any substance or agent that causes cancer (e.g., cigarette smoke, UV radiation, asbestos)
🧠 Memory Trick — Benign vs. Malignant

"Benign = Be-NICE (stays put) · Malignant = MEAN (spreads and invades)"
Benign tumors are nice — they stay in one place. Malignant tumors are mean — they invade and spread everywhere.

🧠 Memory Trick — Metastasis

"META = MOVE" — Metastasis = cancer cells MOVE to new locations. Like a weed spreading seeds to other parts of the garden. Once cancer metastasizes, it's much harder to treat (Stage 4).

📊 Cancer Staging — Stages 1–4
⚠️ Major Exam Topic — Professor will give scenarios!

The professor says: "If I give you a scenario and describe where we are finding tumor cells, can you tell me if it is Stage 1, 2, 3, or 4?" Practice this with the staging table!

Stage Where is the Cancer? Prognosis Memory Hook
Stage 1 Small tumor, confined to original site — has NOT spread to lymph nodes or other organs Best — most curable "Staying put" — tumor is small and local
Stage 2 Larger tumor, still mostly local, may have spread to nearby lymph nodes Good with treatment "Starting to spread" — nearby lymph nodes involved
Stage 3 Tumor has spread to more lymph nodes and possibly nearby tissues/organs More serious "Spreading seriously" — regional spread
Stage 4 Cancer has metastasized — spread to distant organs (liver, lungs, brain, bones) Most serious; hardest to treat "Spread everywhere" — distant metastasis
🧠 Memory Trick — Staging

"1 = Local · 2 = Nearby nodes · 3 = Regional spread · 4 = Far away"
Think of it like a fire: Stage 1 = spark in one room · Stage 2 = spread to hallway · Stage 3 = whole floor · Stage 4 = whole building on fire

💊 Cancer Treatments
⭐ Know All 3 Treatment Categories
Treatment What It Does Best For Key Side Effects / Limits
1. Surgery Physically removes the tumor from the body Solid tumors that haven't spread (Stage 1–2); the ONLY curative therapy for many solid tumors Works best when there are NO distant metastases and no local infiltration
2. Radiation Uses ionizing radiation (X-rays or gamma rays) to damage cancer cell DNA and kill them Tumors sensitive to radiation; often combined with surgery Fatigue, skin reactions, GI issues; radiation itself is mutagenic (can cause secondary cancers)
3. Chemotherapy Uses drugs to halt cell division throughout the body Cancers that have spread (metastasized); blood cancers (leukemia) Kills fast-dividing cells — including cancer AND normal cells (hair follicles, gut lining) → hair loss, nausea, fatigue. Every chemo drug has some effect on normal tissue.
🧠 Memory Trick — 3 Treatments

"CUT · BURN · POISON" = Surgery · Radiation · Chemotherapy
Surgeons CUT out the tumor. Radiation BURNS the cancer cells with energy. Chemo POISONS fast-dividing cells.

⭐ Emerging Cancer Therapies (from textbook)
  • Angiogenesis inhibitors (angiostatin, endostatin) — block blood vessel growth to tumors, starving them of nutrients
  • Telomerase inhibitors — block the enzyme that lets cancer cells divide indefinitely
  • E2F inhibitors — target cells with defective Rb protein
  • Immunotherapy — uses the patient's own immune system to attack cancer cells
🎓 Professor's TopHat Questions — Cancer
A patient has cancer cells found in a lymph node near the original tumor site. What stage is this?
✅ Stage 2 — cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes but not to distant organs
What is the difference between a benign and malignant tumor?
✅ Benign = stays in one place, does not invade or spread. Malignant = can invade surrounding tissue and metastasize (spread to distant organs).
Why does chemotherapy cause hair loss?
✅ Chemo kills fast-dividing cells — hair follicle cells divide rapidly, so they are also killed along with cancer cells.
