Chapter 6: Photosynthesis

Light Energy · Chloroplasts · Light Reactions · Calvin Cycle
The Master Equation
Photosynthesis Overall Equation
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light Energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Carbon Dioxide + Water + Light → Glucose + Oxygen
Key Facts
  • Photosynthesis is the process by which solar energy is trapped and stored as chemical energy in the bonds of glucose
  • Endergonic reaction — requires energy input (light) to run
  • Takes place in chloroplasts (found in plant cells and algae)
  • Autotrophs (producers) carry out photosynthesis — essential to sustaining all life on Earth
  • The overall reaction is the reverse of cellular respiration
Plant Anatomy & Chloroplast Structure
Know WHERE each reaction occurs — this is a common exam question
Structure Location Function
Mesophyll cellsMiddle layer of leafMain site of photosynthesis; each cell contains several chloroplasts
StomataPores on leaf surfaceGas exchange — CO₂ enters, O₂ exits; water vapor released
Thylakoid membraneStacked disc membranes inside chloroplastLight-dependent reactions occur here
Granum (pl: grana)Stack of thylakoidsHouses photosystems and the electron transport chain
StromaFluid surrounding thylakoidsCalvin cycle (light-independent reactions) occur here
Light & Photosynthetic Pigments
Key Light Facts
  • The sun emits energy in waves — shorter wavelengths have higher energy than longer wavelengths
  • Different pigments specialize in absorbing different wavelengths
  • Chlorophyll a is the primary photosynthetic pigment — absorbs mostly blue and red light
  • Green light is reflected (not absorbed) — this is why plants appear green
  • A photosystem is a large protein structure in the thylakoid membrane consisting of antenna pigments and a reaction center containing chlorophyll
Stage 1: Light-Dependent Reactions
Definition

Light-dependent reactions = the first stage of photosynthesis. Uses light energy to split water and produce ATP and NADPH. Occurs in the thylakoid membrane.

What Happens — Two Photosystems Working in Sequence

Photosynthesis involves 2 processes: (1) light-dependent — splitting of H₂O to form O₂ and H; (2) light-independent — combining H and CO₂ to form sugar.

Step Photosystem II (PSII) Electron Transport Chain Photosystem I (PSI)
What happens Light hits chlorophyll → water is split → O₂ released → electrons energized Energized electrons pass through carrier molecules → protons pumped → ATP made via chemiosmosis Electrons re-energized by light → NADP⁺ reduced → forms NADPH
Products O₂ (released) + energized electrons ATP (via ATP synthase) NADPH
Note — Photosystem numbering

Photosystem II comes before Photosystem I in the actual sequence. They were named in the order they were discovered, not the order they work.

Key Terms — Light Reactions
  • Chemiosmosis = protons flow through ATP synthase to generate ATP
  • ATP synthase = the protein channel protons flow through; makes ATP as they pass
  • NADPH = energy carrier that carries hydrogen atoms to the Calvin cycle
  • Photolysis = splitting of water by light energy in PSII
Stage 2: The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
Definition

Calvin Cycle = the second stage of photosynthesis. Uses ATP and NADPH from the light reactions to convert CO₂ into glucose. Occurs in the stroma.

Reactants & Products
REACTANTS (in):
  • CO₂ (from air through stomata)
  • ATP (from light reactions)
  • NADPH (from light reactions)
PRODUCTS (out):
  • G3P → Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)
  • ADP + Pᵢ (returned to light reactions)
  • NADP⁺ (returned to light reactions)
The 3 Steps of the Calvin Cycle
  1. Carbon Fixation: CO₂ is attached to RuBP (5-carbon) by the enzyme RuBisCO → makes a 6-carbon compound that immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules (3-PGA)
  2. Reduction: ATP and NADPH are used to convert 3-PGA into G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate) — the actual sugar product
  3. Regeneration of RuBP: Most G3P is used to regenerate RuBP (using ATP) so the cycle can continue; some G3P exits to make glucose
Role of ATP and NADPH in the Calvin Cycle

ATP provides the energy and NADPH provides the hydrogen atoms needed to reduce CO₂ into G3P (sugar). Both come from the light reactions and are recycled back as ADP and NADP⁺ when the Calvin cycle runs.

Chapter 7: Cellular Respiration

How Cells Release Energy · Glycolysis · Krebs Cycle · ETC · Fermentation
The Master Equation
Cellular Respiration Overall Equation
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 36 ATP
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy (ATP)
Key Facts
  • Molecules in food (such as glucose) are used to produce ATP — the energy carrier used to power cell activities
  • Cells cannot survive without ATP
  • Exergonic reaction — releases energy stored in glucose as ATP
  • Aerobic cellular respiration is used by all plants and animals, as well as many microbes
  • Occurs in the cytoplasm + mitochondria
  • Do not confuse with breathing — cellular respiration is a chemical process inside cells

Stage 1: Glycolysis — Splitting Glucose
Definition

Glycolysis = "glucose splitting" — the breakdown of 1 glucose molecule into 2 pyruvate molecules. Occurs in the cytoplasm. Does not require oxygen. Happens in all cells (aerobic and anaerobic). "Glycolysis means splitting of glucose."

Glycolysis Summary
REQUIRES:
  • 1 glucose
  • 2 NAD⁺
  • 2 ADP
YIELDS:
  • 2 pyruvate
  • 2 NADH (electron carriers)
  • 2 ATP (net gain)
Stage 2: Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA (Transition Step)
What Happens
  • Each pyruvate (3-carbon) loses a CO₂ → becomes a 2-carbon acetyl group
  • The acetyl group attaches to CoA → forms Acetyl-CoA
  • NAD⁺ is reduced to NADH
  • CO₂ is released (part of the CO₂ you exhale)
  • Occurs twice — once per pyruvate (2 pyruvates from glycolysis)
  • Location: mitochondrial matrix
Stage 3: Krebs Cycle
Definition

Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle) = a series of reactions in the mitochondrial matrix that breaks down Acetyl-CoA, releasing CO₂ and producing electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂) and a small amount of ATP.

Krebs Cycle — Per 2 Acetyl-CoA (both turns of the cycle)
REACTANTS:
  • 2 Acetyl-CoA (2-carbon each)
  • Oxaloacetate (recycled — the "starter")
PRODUCTS:
  • 4 CO₂ (released as gas)
  • 6 NADH (electron carriers)
  • 2 FADH₂ (electron carriers)
  • 2 ATP
CO₂ is produced in TWO places

CO₂ is released during: (1) the Transition step (pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA) AND (2) the Krebs cycle. This CO₂ eventually diffuses out and is exhaled.

Stage 4: Electron Transport Chain (ETC)
Definition

Electron Transport Chain = a series of protein complexes in the inner mitochondrial membrane. NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons, which release energy used to pump protons (H⁺) across the membrane, driving ATP synthesis via chemiosmosis.

  1. NADH and FADH₂ donate their electrons to the ETC
  2. Electrons pass through protein complexes, releasing energy at each step
  3. Energy pumps H⁺ ions into the intermembrane space (building up concentration)
  4. H⁺ ions flow back through ATP synthase → produces 34 ATP
  5. At the end of the chain, electrons + H⁺ combine with O₂ → H₂O (water)
What happens without O₂?

Without O₂, electrons cannot be accepted at the end of the ETC → the chain stops → NADH cannot be recycled → fermentation occurs instead.

Fermentation — When O₂ Is Not Available
Definition

Fermentation = an anaerobic process that regenerates NAD⁺ from NADH so glycolysis can continue. Generates ATP only in glycolysis (2 ATP total). Occurs in the cytoplasm.

Type What Happens Products Example
Alcoholic fermentation NADH reduces pyruvate to ethanol; NAD⁺ is re-created Ethanol + CO₂ Yeast making beer/bread
Lactic acid fermentation NADH reduces pyruvate to lactic acid; NAD⁺ is re-created Lactic acid Muscle cells during intense exercise
Other Food Molecules

Proteins and fats can also enter the energy-extracting pathways — they enter at different points (fats enter as acetyl-CoA; proteins enter as various intermediates).

Chapter 8: Mitosis & Cell Division

Cell Cycle · Binary Fission · Chromosomes · PMAT · Cytokinesis
Overview — Cell Division
Key Concepts
  • Cell division produces a continuous supply of replacement cells
  • Each daughter cell receives a complete set of hereditary information from the parent cell and about half its cytoplasm
  • Asexual reproduction — offspring formed from a single parent; offspring are genetically identical clones
  • Sexual reproduction — offspring produced by fusion of gametes (sperm and egg) from two adults; requires meiosis
  • Mitosis adds new cells; apoptosis (programmed cell death) removes them — allowing tissues to renew themselves
Chromosome Vocabulary — Know the Differences
Must-Know Terms
  • Chromosome = a single, condensed DNA molecule + proteins (histones). Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
  • Chromatin = the loose, uncondensed form of DNA + histone proteins (what chromosomes look like during interphase)
  • Nucleosome = DNA wrapped around 8 histone proteins — the basic unit of chromatin packaging
  • Sister chromatids = the two identical copies of a chromosome joined at the centromere after DNA replication
  • Homologous chromosomes = a pair of chromosomes — one from mom, one from dad — that carry genes for the same traits but may have different versions (alleles). NOT identical copies.
  • Centromere = the region where sister chromatids are joined; also the attachment site for spindle microtubules
  • Telomeres = regions at the ends of chromosomes essential for chromosome stability
  • Diploid (2n) = having 2 sets of chromosomes. Human body cells = 2n = 46.
  • Haploid (n) = having 1 set of chromosomes. Human gametes (sperm/egg) = n = 23.
  • Alleles = enduring mutations inherited generation after generation; different versions of a gene (e.g., black, brown, or blond hair)
Sister Chromatids vs. Homologous Chromosomes — Common Exam Confusion
Sister Chromatids Homologous Chromosomes
IDENTICAL copies of the same chromosomeSame chromosome type but from different parents — may have different alleles
Joined at the centromereNOT joined — separate chromosomes
Created by DNA replication (S phase)Inherited — one from mom, one from dad
Separated during ANAPHASE of mitosisSeparated during meiosis I (not mitosis)
Human Chromosomes
  • A typical human cell has 23 pairs of chromosomes, for a total of 46
  • 22 pairs = autosomes
  • 1 pair = sex chromosomes (different in males and females)
Binary Fission — Prokaryote Division
Definition

Binary fission = how prokaryotes (bacteria) reproduce. An asexual process that replicates DNA and distributes it to two daughter cells. Much simpler than mitosis — prokaryotes have one circular chromosome and no nucleus.

Binary Fission Steps
  1. The circular chromosome is replicated; DNA attaches to the cell membrane
  2. The cell membrane grows between the two DNA molecules
  3. The cell elongates
  4. The cell divides in half, forming two identical daughter cells
The Cell Cycle
Cell Cycle vs. Mitosis — They Are NOT the Same

Cell cycle = the entire life of a cell (interphase + mitosis + cytokinesis)
Mitosis = just the nuclear division phase of the cell cycle (PMAT)

Phase Stage What Happens
INTERPHASE G₁ (Gap 1) Cell grows; makes proteins and organelles; normal cell activities. Protein synthesis occurs.
S (Synthesis) DNA is replicated — every chromosome is copied; cell now has double the DNA
G₂ (Gap 2) Cell continues to grow; produces proteins necessary for mitosis; DNA begins to wind more tightly around histones
MITOSIS PMAT (4 phases) Nucleus divides; chromosomes are separated into 2 identical sets
CYTOKINESIS After telophase Cytoplasm divides; 2 daughter cells are formed
G₀ Phase

G₀ = the cell continues its function but does not replicate its DNA or divide. Most cells in your body are currently in G₀.

Chromosome Number in G₂

If an organism has 36 chromosomes in G₂, the diploid number is still 36. In G₂, DNA has been replicated (72 chromatids total) but the chromosome NUMBER is still 36 — each chromosome has 2 sister chromatids joined at the centromere. Count centromeres = count chromosomes.

The 4 Phases of Mitosis — PMAT
Phase What You See Key Events
PROPHASE Chromosomes condense and become visible; nuclear envelope breaks down Duplicated chromosomes condense · Spindle microtubules form from centrioles · Nuclear envelope breaks down · Nucleolus disappears · Chromosomes captured by spindle microtubules (at kinetochores)
METAPHASE Chromosomes line up in the middle of the cell Chromosomes align along the cell's equator (metaphase plate) · Spindle fibers attach to centromeres · Best phase to count chromosomes
ANAPHASE Sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles Centromeres split · Sister chromatids separate and become independent daughter chromosomes · Motor proteins in kinetochores pull chromatids apart · Cell elongates
TELOPHASE + Cytokinesis Two new nuclei form; cell splits in two Nuclear envelopes re-form around each set of chromosomes · Chromosomes uncoil back to chromatin · Spindle fibers disintegrate · Nucleoli reappear · Cytokinesis begins
Cytokinesis — Splitting the Cytoplasm
Feature Animal Cells Plant Cells
Mechanism Cleavage furrow — a ring of microfilaments contracts and pinches the cell in two Cell plate forms in the middle and grows outward to divide the cell
Why different? Animal cells are flexible — can be pinched Plant cells have a rigid cell wall — cannot be pinched; must build a new wall
Cell Cycle Checkpoints

Chemical checkpoints regulate the cell cycle. These checkpoints ensure DNA is properly replicated and the cell is ready before proceeding to the next phase.

Chapter 8: Cancer

What It Is · How It Makes You Sick · Staging · Treatments
What Is Cancer?
Definition

Cancer = a disease of uncontrolled cell growth. Caused by genetic mutations that regulate cell division. Close to 40% of Americans will be diagnosed with cancer.

Key Facts
  • Cancer is caused by mutations in genes that regulate cell division
  • Most cancers require multiple mutations to develop
  • Cancer cells do not respond to normal cell cycle checkpoints
  • Cancer cells can be immortal — they keep dividing indefinitely
How Cancer Cells Differ from Normal Cells
Normal Cells Cancer Cells
Communicate via chemical signals that influence cell division and program cell deathLack effective communication
Stop dividing at a certain density (density-dependent inhibition)Keep dividing regardless of density
Only divide a certain number of timesAre not programmed to die (can be immortal)
Must be anchored to divideDo not need to be anchored
Tumor vs. Cancer — Not Interchangeable
  • Tumor (neoplasm) = an abnormal growth of tissue capable of forming a mass
  • Benign tumor = does NOT invade surrounding tissue and does NOT spread — not cancer
  • Malignant tumor = CAN invade surrounding tissue and spread to other parts of the body — IS cancer
How Cancer Makes You Sick
Health Consequences
  • Tumor growth — tumor grows and impairs organ function
  • Metastasis = cancer cells enter the bloodstream, producing secondary tumors in distant organs. This is what makes cancer deadly.
  • Angiogenesis = blood vessels are routed to the tumor, depriving normal cells of oxygen and nutrients
Two Types of Cancer-Causing Gene Mutations
Gene Type Normal Function When Mutated
Proto-oncogene → Oncogene Promotes cell division when needed (like a gas pedal) Stuck in the "ON" position → cell divides non-stop even without a signal
Tumor Suppressor Gene Stops cell division when needed (like brakes) Brakes are broken → cell cannot stop dividing
Specific Tumor Suppressor Proteins — High Yield
Protein Normal Function Cancer Connection
p53 Inspects DNA integrity; stops cell division if DNA is damaged; triggers DNA repair or apoptosis 50% of ALL cancers have a disabled p53. 70–80% of lung cancers have mutant p53.
Rb (Retinoblastoma protein) Blocks E2F protein, which prevents DNA copying. When Rb is released, E2F enables DNA replication. 40% of ALL cancers have a defective Rb. Without Rb, E2F is always active → constant DNA replication.
Telomerase inhibitor Inhibits telomerase → chromosomes lose telomere tips with each division → limits cell to ~30 divisions Almost ALL cancers destroy the telomerase inhibitor → cancer cells divide indefinitely
Key Vocabulary
  • Metastasis = cancer cells break away from the original tumor, enter the bloodstream or lymph system, and form new tumors elsewhere
  • Angiogenesis = tumors send signals to grow new blood vessels toward themselves
  • Apoptosis = programmed cell death — a normal process; cancer cells often disable it
  • Carcinogen = any substance or agent that causes cancer (e.g., cigarette smoke, UV radiation)
Cancer Staging — Stages 1–4
Exam Question — Given a scenario, identify the stage

Often staged based on tumor size, number of lymph nodes involved, and the number of distant metastases. Ranges from 1–4. The lower the number, the better the prognosis.

Stage Where Is the Cancer? Prognosis
Stage 1Small tumor, confined to original site — has NOT spread to lymph nodes or other organsBest — most curable
Stage 2Larger tumor, still mostly local, may have spread to nearby lymph nodesGood with treatment
Stage 3Tumor has spread to more lymph nodes and possibly nearby tissues or organsMore serious
Stage 4Cancer has metastasized — spread to distant organs (liver, lungs, brain, bones)Most serious; hardest to treat
Diagnosis Methods
  • CT scan
  • MRI
Cancer Treatments
Treatment What It Does Key Facts
1. Surgery Physically removes the tumor First modality used successfully in cancer treatment. Only curative therapy for many common solid tumors. Works best when there are no distant metastases and no local infiltration.
2. Radiation Ionizing radiation (X-rays or gamma rays) damages cancer cell DNA Success depends on sensitivity difference between tumor and normal tissue. Side effects: fatigue, skin reactions, GI toxicity. Radiation is itself mutagenic, carcinogenic, and teratogenic — can cause secondary cancers.
3. Chemotherapy Drugs that halt cell division throughout the body Types include alkylating agents, antimetabolites, antitumor antibiotics, plant alkaloids, hormonal agents, and immunotherapy. Every chemotherapeutic has some effect on normal tissue. Kills fast-dividing cells — including hair follicles and gut lining → hair loss, nausea, fatigue.

Memory Tricks — Optional

Use these if they help — skip them if you don't need them
Chapter 6 — Where Photosynthesis Happens
"THYLAKOID = LIGHT  ·  STROMA = SUGAR"
Light reactions happen in the thylakoid membrane. The Calvin cycle (sugar-making) happens in the stroma. Light hits the thylakoid → energy goes to stroma → glucose is made.
Chapter 6 — Calvin Cycle Steps
"CATCH · REDUCE · RECYCLE"
1. CATCH the CO₂ (carbon fixation by RuBisCO)   2. REDUCE it to G3P using ATP + NADPH   3. RECYCLE the RuBP so the cycle keeps going
Chapter 8A — Cell Cycle Order
"I Pee on the MAT"
Interphase → Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase → Cytokinesis
Chapter 8A — Sister Chromatids vs. Homologues
"Sisters = Identical Twins  ·  Homologues = Fraternal Twins"
Sister chromatids = identical twins (exact same DNA, joined at the centromere). Homologous chromosomes = fraternal twins (same chromosome type, but from different parents — similar but not identical).
Chapter 8B — p53 and Rb
"p53 = Police  ·  Rb = Red light"
p53 is the DNA police — checks for damage and arrests the cell cycle. Rb is a red light — stops the cell from copying DNA. When either is broken, the cell runs red lights and ignores the police → cancer.
Chapter 8B — The 3 Cancer Treatments
"CUT · BURN · POISON"
Surgery = CUT out the tumor. Radiation = BURN the cancer cells with ionizing energy. Chemotherapy = POISON fast-dividing cells.
